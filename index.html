About

Rigor, and the Search for Pattern

A result isn't finished when it's stated — it's finished when it survives someone checking it carefully. I'm drawn to problems where that check is the hard part.

My coursework has reached graduate complex analysis alongside honors probability and real analysis. The same instinct runs through my research: I'm drawn to problems where the answer isn't settled, and to the unglamorous work of checking whether a claimed answer holds up.

For my thesis-track research I'm attacking two open problems on finite Blaschke products and inscribed ellipses — but only after auditing the literature to confirm nobody had quietly solved them first. In the Fitzpatrick Lab I benchmark reinforcement-learned quantum control on Dartmouth's HPC cluster, where my contribution has been to distrust the summary statistics: I showed the outcomes are a mixture rather than a bell curve, and that a "clean" group result was an artifact of how the data was conditioned.

Teaching is an extension of the same work. As a Physics TA and lead tutor for Dartmouth's Emerging Engineers program, I try to be the instructor I needed when I was first learning — one who insists that understanding is possible, not just memorization.

Blaschke Products, Poncelet's Theorem, and the Numerical Range

The Blaschke ellipse, the Poncelet closure theorem, and the numerical range of a matrix turn out to describe the same object. My thesis-track research pushes the degree-3 picture higher: whether the Blaschke curve of a degree-6 product is always an ellipse (via the Kippenhahn/Darboux criterion), and explicit odd-order Poncelet ellipses. Before committing a research year, I audited the field's citation graph through 2024–2025 to confirm both problems were genuinely still open. Related work was presented with the Polymath Jr group at JMM 2026.

Benchmarking reinforcement-learned quantum control

I benchmark model-free reinforcement learning for preparing bosonic quantum states, scored against a time-optimal reference, on Dartmouth's HPC cluster. Running a 40-seed sweep, I found the outcomes are a mixture distribution, not a Gaussian — a fraction of seeds freeze at the do-nothing baseline, a failure the reported mean hides. I also showed a "tight, unimodal" result was an artifact of post-selection, and reproduced an internal benchmark to machine precision while flagging its headline as not reproducible as documented. The seed-sweep toolkit is open-source.

Qublitz — Virtual Qubit Simulator

A browser-based quantum lab — Bloch-sphere visualization, microwave-pulse simulation, IQ mixing — built within the Fitzpatrick Lab. My contribution was the QuBlitz Arena game — its mechanics and play — and the physics engine beneath it: I implemented and unit-tested the underlying quantum mechanics (phase interference, Bell-pair entanglement) against a passing automated test suite, so the simulation stays numerically honest.
